What Makes It Special

A love letter to golden-age golf architecture. Every hole is a "template" inspired by the greatest designs of C.B. Macdonald and Seth Raynor — Redan, Biarritz, Cape, Eden, Punchbowl, and more. The fairways are absurdly wide (some 80+ yards), but the green complexes are the most contoured at the resort. You'll have 40-foot putts that break 15 feet. This is the longest course at Bandon and plays into the teeth of the wind on the back nine.

Fun Facts

  • Named after Charles Blair Macdonald, who built the first 18-hole course in America (Chicago Golf Club, 1895) and pioneered "template" hole design.
  • Features the largest greens at the resort — some are over 15,000 sq ft with dramatic contours and false fronts. The Biarritz green on #3 is roughly 100 yards deep.
  • The fairways are enormously wide, giving a "play it as you see it" feel. There are multiple routes to every green depending on wind and pin position.
  • Tom Doak rated it a 9 on his own scale (the Doak Scale) — a rarity, as he gives very few courses above a 7.
  • The Ghost Tree, a lone dead snag on the 16th hole, is one of the most iconic landmarks at the resort.

Hole-by-Hole Tips

#1 — Par 4, 419 yds: Wide-open par 4 to a massive green. The green has a big ridge through the middle — try to land on the same side as the pin or you're looking at a 3-putt.
#3 — Par 3, 185 yds (Biarritz): The green is enormous with a deep swale through the middle. If the pin is on the far side of the swale, you need to carry it. Short is dead — the ball funnels into the valley.
#4 — Par 3, 225 yds (Redan): Classic Redan — aim right and let the slope feed the ball left toward the pin. Fighting the slope here never works. Take one extra club; it plays long.
#5 — Par 4, 413 yds: The fairway is 80+ yards wide. Aim at the right half — the green opens up from that angle and the bunkers are all left.
#6 — Par 5, 560 yds (Cape): The tee shot is over a sandy waste area. Take only as much of the waste as you can comfortably carry. The green rewards a right-side approach. Reachable in two if the wind is helping.
#8 — Par 4, 365 yds: Short par 4 with a huge double green shared with #14. The approach shot is all about choosing the right landing zone — the green has three distinct tiers.
#10 — Par 4, 447 yds: This is where the wind usually hits you. Long par 4 playing into the prevailing breeze. Take driver and aim for the left side of the wide fairway. Par is a great score here.
#12 — Par 4, 340 yds (Short): Drivable par 4 downwind. The green is protected by deep bunkers, but if you catch it right you could be putting for eagle. Layup to 80 yards is the safe play.
#14 — Par 5, 550 yds (Double Plateau): This massive green has two distinct tiers with a 4-foot ledge between them. Being on the wrong tier can mean a 100-foot putt. Ask your caddie which tier the pin is on before your approach.
#15 — Par 3, 135 yds (Punchbowl): Short par 3 to a punchbowl green. The slopes funnel everything toward the center. Aim at the middle and let the contours do the work. Don't overthink this one.
#17 — Par 3, 150 yds (Eden): The green slopes from back-right to front-left. If the pin is back-right, you must carry the front bunker. Otherwise, play safe to the left opening and take your par.
#18 — Par 4, 424 yds: Strong finisher. The fairway doglegs gently right with bunkers guarding the turn. A solid drive to the left-center sets up a mid-iron approach to a well-bunkered green. Don't miss right — it's a tough up-and-down.

What Makes It Special

Consistently ranked the #2 public course in America. Tom Doak's masterpiece weaves through towering dunes and along dramatic ocean bluffs. The stretch of holes 4–6 and 11–13 along the cliff edge are among the most spectacular in golf. Doak intentionally left areas of natural dune grass and gorse, giving the course a rugged, timeless feel. This is pure links golf — ground game, imagination, and managing the wind. We play it twice, so use Rd 2 as a scouting round and attack in Rd 6.

Fun Facts

  • Ranked #2 Public Course in America by Golf Digest (2025-2026) — consistently elite.
  • Tom Doak walked the site for two years before routing the course. He wanted every hole to feel inevitable, like it was always meant to be there.
  • The 13th hole is a short par 4 perched on the cliff edge — one of the most photographed holes in the world. Big hitters can drive the green, but the miss right is the Pacific Ocean.
  • Doak intentionally left many areas of native gorse and dunegrass, giving the course a wilder, more natural look than any other at the resort.
  • The course was built for just $2.2 million — a fraction of what modern courses cost. Proof that great architecture doesn't need a massive budget.

Hole-by-Hole Tips

#1 — Par 4, 366 yds: Gentle opener that doglegs right. A 3-wood or hybrid off the tee is plenty. The green is large with subtle breaks — focus on hitting the fairway and finding the right tier.
#2 — Par 5, 534 yds: Long, sweeping par 5 that doglegs right. The smart play is three good shots to the green — the bunkers guarding the front are deep and penalizing. Layup to your favorite wedge distance.
#4 — Par 4, 463 yds: Brute of a par 4 along the ocean. Into the wind, this plays like a par 5. Take par and move on. Bail out left of the green if you're between clubs — right is dead.
#5 — Par 5, 540 yds: Dramatic par 5 along the bluff. The fairway tilts right toward the ocean on the second shot. Stay left with your layup. The green is perched on the cliff edge and doesn't hold long approaches.
#6 — Par 3, 143 yds: Short but lethal. The green is tiny and perched above the cliff. Play for the center — anything right is gone into the gorse or over the cliff. The wind swirls through this gap. Take an extra club.
#7 — Par 4, 397 yds: Inland hole that doglegs left. The tee shot needs to find the right side of the fairway to open up the approach. The green has a big false front — be sure to carry your approach to the putting surface.
#10 — Par 4, 338 yds: Short, strategic par 4. You can hit anything from driver to 6-iron off the tee depending on wind. The green complex is severe — below the hole is key. Putting from above is nearly impossible to stop near the cup.
#11 — Par 3, 148 yds: Stunning downhill par 3 to an amphitheater green surrounded by dunes. The wind funnels through here unpredictably. Take the safe line to the middle of the green and take your two-putt par.
#12 — Par 4, 401 yds: Beautiful par 4 running along the cliff. The fairway is generous but the approach is all about distance control. The green is shallow and slopes toward the ocean. Missing long or right is a lost ball.
#13 — Par 4, 337 yds: THE signature hole. Short par 4 on the cliff. Big hitters can go for the green but the miss right is the Pacific Ocean. A layup to the flat area at 80–100 yards is the smart play. The green tilts hard right-to-left.
#14 — Par 4, 327 yds: Another short, drivable par 4. The green sits in a punchbowl — you can run the ball along the ground and let the contours funnel it toward the hole. This is links golf at its best.
#16 — Par 4, 338 yds: Uphill par 4 with a blind tee shot over the dunes. The green is built into a hillside and runs away from you. Land your approach short and below the hole. Anything above the hole is a nightmare putt.
#17 — Par 3, 208 yds: Long par 3 playing back toward the ocean. The green is wide but shallow with a steep falloff behind. Club selection is critical — this hole plays 1–2 clubs different depending on wind direction.
#18 — Par 4, 401 yds: Strong finishing hole playing uphill. A good drive to the left side of the fairway gives the best angle into the green. The putting surface has a big backstop — don't be afraid to use it.

What Makes It Special

The newest championship course at Bandon and the only one where every single hole has an ocean view. There are zero bunkers on the entire course — the only defense is wind, massive undulating greens, and the cliff edges. Coore & Crenshaw designed it to feel like a throwback to the earliest days of links golf, where the land dictated the routing. The greens are enormous with wild internal contours — being on the right tier matters more than being close to the pin. This is the most wide-open, free-flowing course you'll ever play.

Fun Facts

  • Zero bunkers on the entire course — the only Bandon course that can claim this. Wind and green contours are the sole defense.
  • Every single hole has a view of the Pacific Ocean. No other course at the resort can say that.
  • The land was originally purchased by Mike Keiser in 2000 and played informally for almost 20 years as a rugged, unfinished course before the official Coore & Crenshaw redesign opened in 2020.
  • Some greens are over 12,000 sq ft with 5+ feet of internal elevation change. A putt on the wrong tier can be 80+ feet.
  • The routing follows the cliff edge for the majority of the round, with the ocean roaring below. It's the most dramatic ocean walk at the resort.
  • The "no bunkers" philosophy was a deliberate homage to early Scottish links courses where sand pits were natural features, not constructed hazards.

Hole-by-Hole Tips

#1 — Par 4, 380 yds: Welcoming opener with a wide fairway. Hit driver and let it roll. The green is large — two-putt par is a great start. Get your sea legs here.
#3 — Par 4, 350 yds: Short par 4 along the ocean. The green is huge with big slopes. Being on the right tier is far more important than being close to the pin. Ask your caddie about pin position.
#4 — Par 5, 530 yds: Reachable par 5 running parallel to the coast. The green is massive and three-putts are common. Focus on getting to the correct tier rather than firing at the flag.
#5 — Par 3, 185 yds: Right on the cliff edge. The wind is always swirling here. Take at least one extra club and aim for the center of the green. Don't short-side yourself — there's room to miss long-left.
#6 — Par 4, 440 yds: Long par 4 into the prevailing wind. This can play 480+. Hit the biggest drive you can and don't be ashamed to hit 5-iron or hybrid into the green. Par is an excellent score.
#7 — Par 5, 530 yds: Beautiful par 5 with the ocean running down the entire left side. Stay right off the tee — there's more room than it looks. The green sits on a shelf with a steep falloff left.
#9 — Par 4, 360 yds: Short par 4 with an enormous green. The putting surface has a huge spine running through it. Land on the correct side. From the wrong side, you'll be putting up and over a mountain.
#11 — Par 4, 370 yds: Turning back toward the ocean. The green is perched on the cliff with a steep drop left. Favor the right side on your approach. A bogey from the left is actually a good recovery.
#13 — Par 3, 165 yds: One of the more dramatic par 3s at the resort. The green slopes hard from right to left toward the ocean. Aim right of center and let gravity bring it toward the pin.
#15 — Par 4, 410 yds: Dramatic approach shot back toward the ocean. The green slopes hard left-to-right, so aim at the left edge and let it feed. Don't go right — it runs off the green quickly.
#16 — Par 3, 135 yds: Short par 3 with a massive green. Figure out which tier the pin is on before choosing your club — the difference between tiers can be 2–3 clubs. This is about precision, not power.
#17 — Par 4, 395 yds: Penultimate hole running along the cliff. The fairway tilts right, so aim left off the tee. The green is wide but shallow — distance control on your approach is everything.
#18 — Par 4, 400 yds: Great finishing hole. The green is tucked behind a rise — you can't see the putting surface on your approach. Trust your yardage and commit. A solid par here is a great way to end the round.

What Makes It Special

The course that started it all. David McLay Kidd's first design in America (and only his second ever) put Bandon on the map in 1999. The routing features dramatic elevation changes between ocean bluffs and inland dunes. It has the highest slope rating at the resort (152) and plays tough, especially in the wind. The stretch from 11 through 16 along the cliff is unforgettable. This is the most "traditional" links course at Bandon — if you've dreamed of playing links golf, this is the experience.

Fun Facts

  • David McLay Kidd was just 27 years old when he designed it. It was his first US design and only his second course ever.
  • Mike Keiser discovered this stretch of Oregon coastline on a tip from Howard McKee, a local who ran McKee's Pub (where we're eating Monday night).
  • Has the highest slope rating (152) and course rating (74.1) at the resort — it's the toughest test from the back tees.
  • The routing was redesigned multiple times before construction. The final version maximizes ocean exposure on the back nine.
  • Consistently ranked among the top 25 public courses in America by every major publication.

Hole-by-Hole Tips

#1 — Par 4, 363 yds: Dogleg right opener. The fairway is generous but the green sits above you. Take enough club on your approach — everything short rolls back down the false front.
#4 — Par 4, 405 yds: Slight dogleg left. The ocean appears on the left for the first time. Aim right-center off the tee — the wind usually pushes left here. The green is well-protected by bunkers.
#5 — Par 5, 519 yds: Reachable in two for big hitters, but the green sits above you and slopes back-to-front. Layup to 80–100 yards for an easier approach angle. Three good shots = birdie putt.
#6 — Par 3, 155 yds: First par 3 on the course. The green is set into the dunes with bunkers front-left and right. Middle of the green is always the smart play. Don't chase a back pin — long is dead.
#8 — Par 4, 336 yds: Short par 4 — driver may be too much. A controlled tee shot to the flat landing area sets up a wedge in. The green has a subtle back-to-front slope that makes putting tricky.
#11 — Par 4, 371 yds: One of the most scenic holes on the course. The fairway tumbles downhill toward the ocean. Club down off the tee — you don't need driver here. The green is framed by the Pacific behind it.
#12 — Par 3, 165 yds: Exposed par 3 along the bluff. Wind is almost always a factor. Take one extra club and aim for the fat part of the green. Missing right is a steep drop; left is a deep bunker.
#13 — Par 5, 528 yds: Par 5 playing along the cliff edge. The ocean is right the entire way. Stay left off the tee and with your layup. The green is narrow and doesn't hold long shots. Three-shot approach is smart.
#15 — Par 4, 428 yds: Long par 4 with an uphill approach. The green is hidden behind a crest. Trust your yardage, take one extra club for the uphill, and swing confidently.
#16 — Par 4, 363 yds: THE signature hole. Dramatic cliff-edge approach with the beach far below on the right. Don't go right — it's a steep drop. The green is wider than it looks. Aim center-left and take your par.
#17 — Par 3, 183 yds: Long par 3 with a deep bunker front-right. The green slopes left, so favor the right side. Into the wind, this can play 210+. Take enough club.
#18 — Par 5, 518 yds: Finishing par 5 playing back toward the clubhouse. Reachable in two if you've bombed your drive. The green is wide but with a big ridge — get on the right side. Birdie chance to finish strong.

What Makes It Special

The most diverse terrain of any course at the resort. Bandon Trails starts in the open dunes, dips into a dense coastal forest mid-round (where you're sheltered from the wind), then bursts back out to expansive dune views on the back nine. It's a completely different golf experience — part links, part parkland, part something entirely unique. Locals and repeat visitors often cite Trails as their favorite course at the resort. The 14th hole, a downhill par 3 emerging from the forest to panoramic views, is one of the most memorable moments at Bandon.

Fun Facts

  • Often called the most underrated course at Bandon — it doesn't get the hype of Pacific Dunes but repeat visitors frequently rank it #1.
  • The routing passes through three distinct ecosystems: open sand dunes, dense shore pine forest, and coastal meadow. No other course at the resort does this.
  • Holes 5–9 run through the forest where you're completely sheltered from the ocean wind. Then #10 bursts back into the open dunes and the wind hits you like a wall.
  • The 14th hole is a dramatic downhill par 3 emerging from the forest canopy to a panoramic ocean dune view. It's arguably the best "reveal" moment at the resort.
  • Bill Coore and Ben Crenshaw also designed Sheep Ranch and Bandon Preserve — they know this land better than any other architects.

Hole-by-Hole Tips

#1 — Par 4, 387 yds: Uphill opener through the dunes. The fairway is generous but the green sits up — take enough club for the elevation change. Don't be short on your approach.
#3 — Par 3, 168 yds: First par 3 in the open dunes. Wind is the factor. The green is wider than it appears. Aim center, adjust for wind, and take your par.
#5 — Par 5, 539 yds: Enters the forest. The hole bends through the trees. Two solid shots to set up a wedge is the smart play. The green is small and well-bunkered. Don't go flag-hunting from 200+ yards.
#6 — Par 4, 387 yds: Beautiful forest hole with a creek crossing. The tee shot needs to avoid the trees right. A fade off the tee is ideal. The green sits in a clearing — a peaceful moment in the round.
#8 — Par 4, 407 yds: Tough par 4 in the forest. The fairway is narrower here with trees on both sides. Hit a controlled tee shot to the center and accept a longer approach. Trying to overpower this hole usually backfires.
#10 — Par 4, 371 yds: Coming out of the trees into the open dunes. The wind will hit you again — recalibrate. The green runs away from you — land your approach short and let it release to the pin.
#12 — Par 5, 556 yds: Long par 5 through the dunes. The wind usually helps here. Bomb a drive, lay up to a comfortable wedge distance, and give yourself a birdie look. The green is receptive.
#14 — Par 3, 138 yds: THE signature hole. Dramatic downhill par 3 emerging from the forest to panoramic dune views. The drop is significant — play 1–2 clubs less than the yardage suggests. Aim for the center; the green is well-guarded but the visual will distract you. Soak it in.
#15 — Par 4, 372 yds: Back in the open dunes. A strong drive here opens up a short approach. The green has a big backstop — don't be afraid to use it. A firm 8-iron that bounces off the back slope can end up close.
#17 — Par 4, 416 yds: Long and tough. Driver to the right side of the fairway avoids the bunkers left. The approach is all carry over a waste area — make sure you have enough club to clear it.
#18 — Par 4, 377 yds: Finishing hole back through the dunes to the clubhouse. The fairway funnels into the green beautifully. A solid drive and a confident approach can set up a closing birdie. Finish strong for the team match.

What Makes It Special

The perfect arrival-day warm-up. 13 holes of pure par-3 golf ranging from 80 to 160 yards, all set on high ground with panoramic ocean views. No pressure, no big swings — just wedges, a 7-iron, and your putter. All net proceeds go to the Wild Rivers Coast Alliance, a local conservation group. It's also the ideal place to settle pre-trip bets and get a feel for the wind before the main event starts Saturday morning.

Fun Facts

  • All 13 holes are par 3s — bring wedges, a 7-iron, and a putter. You won't need anything else.
  • 100% of net proceeds go to the Wild Rivers Coast Alliance, supporting conservation on the southern Oregon coast.
  • The course sits on the highest ground at the resort with 360-degree views of the ocean and dunes.
  • Designed by the same duo that built Trails, Sheep Ranch, and many other world-class courses.
  • No tee times needed in the afternoon — just show up and play. Perfect for our arrival-day schedule.

Tips for Playing

General: Focus on feel and distance control. This is a warm-up round, not a war. Groove your wedge distances and get a feel for how the coastal wind affects short irons. You'll need those skills for the next three days.
#3 (115 yds): Downhill with a view of the ocean. The green slopes hard left — aim right of center and let gravity do the work. One of the prettiest holes on the course.
#8 (155 yds): The longest hole on the course. Usually into the wind, so it plays closer to 170. A smooth 7-iron is better than a hard 8. Don't let the distance tempt you into a swing that's too aggressive.
#11 (90 yds): Short but tricky. The green is tiny with bunkers on both sides. Pick a precise wedge yardage and commit to the shot. Sand wedge with a 3/4 swing is usually perfect.
#13 (120 yds): Finishing hole with the whole group watching. The green is open in front — run one up there and make your putt. Great way to kick off the trip with a birdie.

What Makes It Special

19 holes of pure fun built in the dramatic dunes between Old Macdonald and Bandon Trails. Holes range from about 60 to 145 yards with creative bump-and-run options on nearly every hole. It's designed to be walked quickly with minimal fuss — just grab your wedges and a putter and go. The perfect send-off course on departure day: fast enough to play before heading to the airport, fun enough to settle any remaining bets.

Fun Facts

  • 19 holes — the extra hole means an odd number, so there's always a decisive result if you're playing a match.
  • Built in the dunes between Old Macdonald and Bandon Trails — the terrain is wild and dramatic.
  • Designed by Rod Whitman, who also worked on courses like Cabot Cliffs. He's a master of natural, minimalist design.
  • Holes range from about 60 to 145 yards. Creative bump-and-run options abound — think of it as a short game laboratory.
  • No carts, no caddies needed. Just grab PW, SW, LW, and putter. Leave the bag in the room.

Tips for Playing

General: This is a wedge-and-putter course. Bring PW, SW, LW, and putter — nothing else. It's meant to be played light and fast. Focus on creativity over power. Half the fun is figuring out the best angle to bump the ball into each green.
#3 (130 yds): One of the longer holes with a steep dune backdrop. The safe miss is short — anything long runs away quickly into the dune grass behind the green.
#7 (65 yds): Tiny hole in a natural dune bowl. The green is small but the surroundings funnel the ball in. A firm chip with a sand wedge is all you need. Don't overthink it.
#10 (85 yds): Fun little hole with a big dune on the right. Play a low bump to the opening in front and let it roll up to the pin. Trying to fly it in usually ends in the bunker.
#14 (120 yds): One of the best holes on the course. The green sits in a natural amphitheater of dunes. The wind swirls here — throw some grass up before choosing your club.
#19 (105 yds): Great finishing hole. If the Bandit Cup is on the line, this is where the pressure peaks. The green is visible from the path back — everyone will be watching. Play for the center and make the putt.

May in Bandon: Expect 50–62°F, wind, and weather that changes by the hour.
Layer everything. You may see sun, fog, wind, and rain all in one round.
Bandon Essentials
▶Waterproof rain jacket — Loose enough to swing in with layers underneath.
▶Waterproof rain pants — Non-negotiable. Wind alone justifies these even without rain.
▶Rain gloves — e.g. FootJoy RainGrip. Game-changer in wet/windy rounds.
▶2 pairs of waterproof golf shoes — Alternate on 36-hole days so one can dry. Break them in before the trip.
▶5-6 pairs of moisture-wicking socks — Change between rounds on double days. #1 tip from Bandon veterans.
▶Moleskin / blister pads — 30+ miles of walking in 3 days. Blisters are common.
▶Gold Bond powder — Apply before each round to prevent chafing on long walking days.
▶Ibuprofen — For feet and general soreness after double days.
▶Sunscreen SPF 30+ — Coastal sun reflects off water. You'll burn on overcast days.
▶Lip balm with SPF
▶Hand warmers — A few packets for early morning rounds (can be in the 40s).
▶Beanie / wool hat — For early morning tee times.
▶Zip-lock bags — For phone, wallet, scorecard in downpours.
▶2 bag towels — Keep one dry in a zip-lock.
▶Cash for caddie tips — Refer to Caddie Picks tab for estimate of caddie cash needed.
▶Snacks / energy bars — For mid-round fuel on double days.
Resort provides: Push carts (free), 24hr shuttle, turn stands with food on every course.
